Research question & approach
Central question
Does Airbnb supply expansion harm hotel performance — and if so, through which channels? When is Airbnb a real competitive threat vs. a coexistence partner?
Natural experiment
Charleston's 2018 ordinance banning entire-home STRs creates an exogenous policy shock — a clean instrument for STR supply variation not driven by market demand. Columbia and Greenville serve as unregulated control cities.
Identification strategy
Two-stage instrumental variable (2SLS) combined with two-way fixed effects (city + month-by-year). The policy shock instruments STR supply, eliminating endogeneity bias from demand shocks that jointly drive both Airbnb entry and hotel performance.

Empirical framework — two-stage IV model
First stage — policy as instrument for STR supply
The 2018 Charleston ordinance (Treat × After) instruments ln(STR Supply). Valid instrument requires: (1) relevance — policy significantly reduces STR supply; (2) exclusion — policy affects hotel performance only through STR supply, not through independent demand shocks.
ln(STRSupply)zt = π(Treat × After) + αz + γt + ε
Second stage — effect of STR supply on hotel outcomes
Predicted STR supply from stage 1 is used to estimate causal effect on hotel OCC, ADR, RevPAR, Demand, and Revenue — isolating supply-driven variation from confounding demand shocks.
Yzt = β·ln(STRSupply)̂zt + αz + γt + u
Exclusion restriction — validated via Google Trends
Tourism search interest (flights, hotels, activities, travel) remained parallel across Charleston and control cities before and after Aug 2018. No divergent demand shock at policy date confirms the ordinance is supply-side only.
Why OLS alone is not sufficient
Endogeneity problem
Airbnb supply grows in markets with strong tourism demand — the same demand that also boosts hotel performance. OLS conflates demand-driven hotel growth with Airbnb's competitive effect, biasing estimates toward zero or even positive.
OLS vs. IV comparison — hotel demand
OLS (preferred model): -0.040 — not significant. IV (preferred model): -0.058 — significant at 5%. The attenuation in OLS reflects endogeneity bias. IV recovers the true negative substitution effect by using only policy-driven supply variation.
Model progression — R² improvement
Hotel demand R² rises from 0.002 (no FE) to 0.830 (city + year-month FE). The vast majority of variation explained by fixed effects — confirming that simpler models suffer severe specification error.

Subgroup analysis — entire-home vs. shared/private listings
Entire-home: demand -0.051*, ADR +0.069 insig, occ insig, revenue insig. Shared/private: demand -0.045*, ADR +0.061 insig, occ insig, revenue insig.
Competitive effects on hotel demand are nearly identical across listing types (-0.051 vs -0.045). Policy targeting by listing category produces no meaningfully different hotel outcome — at the aggregate market level.
Robustness checks
Three robustness approaches — all confirm main findings
Performance index (normalized vs. control cities) — OCC, ADR, RevPAR indices show no statistically significant change post-policy. t-statistics all below 1.3. Confirms no material shift in hotel equilibrium.
Level-form dependent variables (not log) — OLS and IV models using raw OCC, ADR, Demand, Revenue values mirror log-form conclusions. Demand shows modest significant decline; pricing and revenue effects remain zero.
Alternative STR supply measures — availability-adjusted property supply and availability-adjusted room supply produce results consistent with baseline. Conclusions not an artifact of supply metric definition.
Performance index — pre vs. post regulation
Pre-policy: OCC index 1.106, ADR index 1.873, RevPAR index 2.079. Post-policy: OCC 1.096, ADR 1.809, RevPAR 1.995. All changes insignificant (p > 0.20).
All changes p>0.20 — statistically indistinguishable from zero. Charleston's hotel performance tracked control cities through the regulatory shock.
Strategic implications — for operators and investors
When Airbnb IS a real threat
Markets with frequent compression nights (>90% occ) — Airbnb absorbs overflow demand that hotels could otherwise price up
High Airbnb penetration (>15% supply share) relative to hotel stock
Economy and midscale segments — Airbnb's price point competes directly; luxury less exposed
When Airbnb is NOT a material threat
Mid-sized tourism markets without frequent capacity constraints — like Charleston (avg 11 full-compression nights/yr)
Hotels with strong brand loyalty, business travel mix, or service differentiation
Markets where STR and hotels serve distinct traveler segments — partial substitution, not direct competition
Limits of STR regulation for hotel benefit
A -43% Airbnb supply shock produced only a -0.06% hotel demand effect. Regulations help platforms less than hotel advocates expect
Zero effect on ADR and revenue — hotels cannot capture pricing upside from STR restrictions in low-compression markets
STR and hotels primarily coexist rather than compete — differentiated market structure persists post-regulation
