Research design & data
Causal identification strategy
Difference-in-differences (DiD)
Compares Charleston (treated) vs. Columbia & Greenville (control) before and after Aug 2018 enforcement. Two-way fixed effects: postal code + month-by-year. Clustered SEs at postal code level (N=30 clusters).
Event study design
Dynamic coefficients from 12 months pre-policy to 56 months post-policy. Validates parallel trends assumption — pre-period coefficients statistically indistinguishable from zero.

Key finding 2 — entry vs. exit dynamics
DiD estimates split by immediate (months 1–6) and long-run (months 7+) post-policy periods
New entry — persistent deterrence
Entry months 1-6: -6.46. Entry months 7+: -6.65.
Effect is immediate and persistent — compliance costs and eligibility requirements durably deter new host participation.
Exit — temporary spike, then stabilization
Exit months 1-6: +3.5. Exit months 7+: -2.4.
Initial spike reflects non-compliant exits. Reversal implies surviving listings are more resilient — policy filtered out marginal hosts.
Supply contraction mechanism — what drove the -44%?
Entry deterrence: primary driver (sustained). Exit spike: secondary driver (transitory).
Dominant mechanism = sustained entry deterrence. Exit spike was real but transitory. Market did not collapse — it contracted and restabilized around compliant incumbents.
Key finding 3 — robustness checks
Strategic evasion — all three channels tested
Colisting (same unit, multiple profiles) — fewer than 50 properties detected system-wide. Not a material confounder.
Within-listing category switching — listing types stable across all Property IDs throughout study period. No observed transitions.
Strategic relisting (delist + relist new category) — 0–0.6% of hosts per year; peaked at 0.613% in 2020. Below 1% in every year.
Low evasion rates confirm findings reflect genuine behavioral adjustment, not data artifacts or regulatory arbitrage.
Pre-pandemic sample robustness
Full sample: Aug 2017 – Apr 2023 (68 months)
Pre-COVID sample: Aug 2017 – Feb 2020 (30 months)
Pre-COVID post-treatment window: 18 months
Result: negative effect persists (smaller magnitude) — confirms regulation drives the reduction, not pandemic
COVID represents a common shock — parallel trends preserved. Pre-pandemic robustness confirms causal interpretation is not COVID-contaminated.
Policy & strategic implications
For policymakers
Categorical restrictions reduce supply meaningfully but create spillover effects that deter even compliant operators. Enforcement credibility depends more on monitoring capacity and platform data-sharing than statutory strictness alone.
For platform operators
Entry deterrence — not forced exit — is the dominant supply-reduction mechanism. Platforms should anticipate structural contraction in regulated markets driven by reduced new supply, not mass incumbent departure.
Methodological lesson
Model specification fundamentally determines causal validity. Without postal code FE, the naive estimate is +115% (sign-reversed). The preferred DiD estimate with granular time controls is -44% — a 159-point swing from misspecification.
